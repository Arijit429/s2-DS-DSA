F01

The Mean (Average)

The single most basic summary of data — the balance point.

Arithmetic Mean
$$\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}=\frac{x_1+x_2+\dots+x_n}{n}$$
Why we need it

Data is a pile of numbers. We want one number that represents the whole pile — the "typical" value. The mean is the value where, if every data point handed over its excess and made up everyone's shortfall, all points would end up equal. It's the balance point of the data.

Where it comes from

If you want to share a total equally among n people, you add everything up and split it into n equal parts. That's literally all the formula says: total, divided by how many. Nothing deeper is hidden in it.

2 Count: $n=4$.

3 Mean $=\dfrac{20}{4}=5$.

F02

Variance & Standard Deviation

The mean tells you the center. This tells you how spread out the data is.

Variance → Standard Deviation
$$\sigma^2=\frac{\sum(x-\mu)^2}{N}\qquad \sigma=\sqrt{\sigma^2}$$
Why we need it

Two classes can both average 70 marks — but one might be every student scoring 68–72, the other half scoring 40 and half scoring 100. The mean can't tell them apart. We need a number for how far values typically sit from the mean. That's variability.

Where it comes from

Natural idea: measure each point's distance from the mean, $(x-\mu)$, and average those distances. But there's a problem — points above the mean give positive distances, points below give negative, and they cancel out to zero every time. Useless.

Fix: square each distance first. Squaring makes everything positive (so nothing cancels), and it also punishes big deviations harder than small ones (being 4 away counts as 16, being 2 away counts as 4). Then we average those squared distances. That average is the variance.

But variance is in "squared units" (squared marks, squared rupees) — awkward to interpret. So we take the square root to get back to the original units. That's the standard deviation — the headline measure of spread.

$\sigma$
Standard deviation

$x-\mu$

find it subtract the mean from each value
$(x-\mu)^2$
Squared deviation

$\mu$

!
Population vs sample — the n−1 question. If your data is a sample (not the whole population), divide by $n-1$ instead of $N$: $s^2=\frac{\sum(x-\bar x)^2}{n-1}$. Reason: you already used the data once to estimate the mean $\bar x$, so one "piece of independent information" is spent. Dividing by $n-1$ corrects for that and stops the formula from underestimating the true spread. Rule of thumb: whole group → N, a sample → n−1.
Try it

Data: 2, 4, 6 (treat as a full population).

1 Mean $\mu=\frac{2+4+6}{3}=4$.

2 Deviations: $-2, 0, +2$. Squared: $4, 0, 4$.

3 Variance $=\frac{4+0+4}{3}=\frac{8}{3}\approx2.67$.

4 Std dev $=\sqrt{2.67}\approx1.63$.

F03

Coefficient of Variation

Spread expressed as a percentage of the mean, so you can compare unlike things.

Coefficient of Variation
$$CV=\frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\times100\%$$
Why we need it

Is a standard deviation of 5 "big"? It depends. 5 around a mean of 10 is huge (half the size). 5 around a mean of 1000 is tiny. Raw standard deviation can't be compared across datasets with different scales or units. CV fixes this by asking: how big is the spread relative to the mean?

Where it comes from

If you divide the spread $(\sigma)$ by the center $(\bar x)$, the units cancel — "marks ÷ marks" leaves a pure number. Multiply by 100 to read it as a percentage. So CV literally says "the spread is X% of the average." Bigger CV = more relative variability.

$\sigma$
Standard deviation
The spread.
find it compute it via F02
$\bar{x}$
Mean
The center.
find it compute it via F01
$100\%$
Convert to percent
Just makes the ratio readable as a percentage.
Try it

Mean $=50$, SD $=10$.

1 $CV=\frac{10}{50}\times100\% = 20\%$. The spread is 20% of the mean.

F04

IQR & the Outlier Rule

A spread measure that ignores extremes, plus how to flag the extremes.

Interquartile Range & Fences
$$IQR=Q_3-Q_1\qquad [\,Q_1-1.5\,IQR,\ \ Q_3+1.5\,IQR\,]$$
Why we need it

The range (max − min) is wrecked by a single freak value. We want a spread measure that looks at the bulk of the data and ignores the extremes. And separately, we want an objective rule to decide what counts as "an extreme" (an outlier) instead of eyeballing it.

Where it comes from

Sort the data and chop it into four equal quarters. The cut points are the quartiles $Q_1$ (25% mark) and $Q_3$ (75% mark). The distance between them, $Q_3-Q_1$, is the spread of the middle 50% — the heart of the data, untouched by outliers.

For the outlier rule, statisticians use one IQR-and-a-half as a "reasonable reach" beyond the middle box. Step that far below $Q_1$ and above $Q_3$ to build fences. Anything beyond the fences is unusually far from the bulk → flagged as an outlier. (The 1.5 is a convention chosen to catch genuine outliers without over-flagging.)

Each term, decoded
$Q_1$
First quartile (25th percentile)

find it sort data; it's the median of the lower half
$Q_3$
Third quartile (75th percentile)
75% of the data is below it.
find it sort data; it's the median of the upper half
$1.5$
The fence multiplier
A standard convention for "how far is too far."
Try it

$Q_1=20$, $Q_3=40$. Is 75 an outlier?

1 $IQR=40-20=20$.

2 Upper fence $=40+1.5(20)=70$.

3 $75>70$ → yes, an outlier.

F05

Expectation E(X)

The "mean" of a random variable — the long-run average outcome.

Expected Value (discrete)
$$E(X)=\sum x\,P(x)$$
Why we need it

When outcomes are random (a dice game, an insurance payout), we want to know: if I repeated this thousands of times, what would the average result be? That long-run average is the expectation. It's how you decide if a bet is worth taking.

Where it comes from

An ordinary average weights every value equally. But random outcomes aren't equally likely — rolling a 6 might be rarer than rolling a 1. So instead of a plain average, we take a weighted average: each outcome is weighted by how probable it is. Multiply each value by its probability, add them up. Frequent outcomes pull the average toward themselves; rare ones barely move it.

Each term, decoded
$E(X)$
Expected value of X
The long-run mean of the random variable.
$x$
A possible outcome value
Each value the random variable can take.
$P(x)$
Probability of that outcome
The weight — how likely $x$ is.
find it from the problem's probability table; all $P(x)$ sum to 1
Try it

A game pays ₹0 with prob 0.5, ₹10 with prob 0.3, ₹100 with prob 0.2.

1 $E(X)=0(0.5)+10(0.3)+100(0.2)$.

2 $=0+3+20=₹23$ on average per play.

F06

Variance of a Random Variable

The shortcut formula — and why it's just the squared-deviation idea again.

Variance — computational form
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-\big[E(X)\big]^2$$
Why we need it

Same reason as before: knowing the average outcome isn't enough — we want to know how risky / spread out the random outcomes are. A lottery and a savings account can have the same expected value but wildly different risk. Variance measures that risk.

Where it comes from

By definition, variance is the expected squared deviation: $E\big[(X-\mu)^2\big]$. If you expand that bracket using algebra and simplify (using $E$'s properties), all the middle terms collapse and you're left with the clean form $E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2$ — "the mean of the squares minus the square of the mean." It's the same concept as F02, just rearranged into the easiest thing to actually compute.

Each term, decoded
$E(X)$
The mean of X
Expected value.
find it $\sum x\,P(x)$ — see F05
$E(X^2)$
The mean of X-squared
Square each outcome first, then take the weighted average.
find it $\sum x^2\,P(x)$ — same table, but use $x^2$
$[E(X)]^2$
The mean, then squared
Take $E(X)$ and square the single number.
!
Don't mix these two up. $E(X^2)$ = square then average. $[E(X)]^2$ = average then square. They are different numbers, and their difference is the variance.
Try it

Given $E(X)=3$ and $E(X^2)=13$.

1 $\operatorname{Var}(X)=13-3^2=13-9=4$.

F07

The Binomial Formula

Probability of getting exactly k successes in n yes/no trials.

Binomial Probability
$$P(X{=}k)=\binom{n}{k}\,p^{k}\,(1-p)^{n-k}$$
Why we need it

Tons of situations are "do something n times, each time it either works or fails": flip a coin 10 times, test 20 products for defects, 5 patients either recover or don't. We want the probability of exactly k successes. Counting by hand gets impossible fast — this formula does it directly.

Where it comes from

Build it from three questions:

1. What's the chance of one specific success pattern? Say k successes then the rest failures. Each success has probability $p$, each failure $(1-p)$, and trials are independent so you multiply: $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$.

2. But the k successes could happen in any order. SSF, SFS, FSS all count. How many orderings are there? That's "choose which k of the n trials are the successes" — the combination $\binom{n}{k}$.

3. Add up all those equally-likely patterns: multiply the count by the per-pattern probability. That's the formula.

Each term, decoded
$n$
Number of trials
How many times you repeat the experiment.
$k$
Number of successes you want
The exact count you're asking about.
$p$
Probability of success on one trial
Stays the same every trial.
find it given in the problem (e.g. fair coin → 0.5)
$1{-}p$
Probability of failure
Whatever's left over from success.
$\binom{n}{k}$
"n choose k" — the combination
Number of ways to arrange k successes among n trials.
find it $\dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$
Try it

Fair coin tossed 4 times. P(exactly 2 heads)? Here $n=4,k=2,p=0.5$.

1 $\binom{4}{2}=\frac{4!}{2!2!}=6$.

2 $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}=(0.5)^2(0.5)^2=0.0625$.

3 $P=6\times0.0625=0.375=\frac{3}{8}$.

F08

The Poisson Formula

Probability of k rare events in a fixed window of time or space.

Poisson Probability
$$P(X{=}k)=\frac{\lambda^{k}\,e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$$
Why we need it

Some things happen at a steady average rate but at random moments: calls to a helpline per hour, machine breakdowns per week, typos per page. You don't have "n trials" — events just arrive. We want the probability of seeing exactly k of them in one window. The Poisson formula handles exactly this.

Where it comes from

Imagine slicing the time window into a huge number of tiny instants. In each instant an event either happens or doesn't — that's secretly a binomial with enormous n and tiny p. If you take the binomial formula and let n → ∞ while keeping the average count $np=\lambda$ fixed, the math simplifies into the Poisson formula. The $e^{-\lambda}$ falls out of that limit (the constant $e$ always shows up in continuous growth/decay limits), and the $k!$ is the leftover of the binomial's combination term.

Each term, decoded
$\lambda$
Lambda — the average rate
Expected number of events in the window. Also equals the variance.
find it given as "average X per period" in the problem
$k$
The exact event count you want
0, 1, 2, … — the number you're asking about.
$e$
Euler's number ≈ 2.718
A fixed math constant; appears from the limiting process.
find it use the $e^x$ button on a calculator
$k!$
k factorial
$k\times(k-1)\times\dots\times1$. Note $0!=1$.
find it e.g. $3!=3\cdot2\cdot1=6$
!
The headline property: for Poisson, the mean and the variance are both equal to λ. This single fact is asked constantly.
Try it

Breakdowns average λ=2 per week. P(exactly 1 breakdown)?

1 $P(1)=\dfrac{2^1 e^{-2}}{1!}=2e^{-2}$.

2 $e^{-2}\approx0.135$, so $P\approx2(0.135)=0.271$.

F09

The Z-Score

How many standard deviations a value sits from the mean.

Standardization
$$Z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$$
Why we need it

"You scored 80" is meaningless alone. 80 out of 100 with class mean 50 is brilliant; 80 with class mean 95 is poor. We need to express a value in terms of how unusual it is relative to its own distribution, on a universal scale that lets you compare scores from totally different tests.

Where it comes from

Two steps. (1) Re-center: subtract the mean, $x-\mu$, so the average becomes 0 and the number tells you how far above/below average you are. (2) Re-scale: divide by the standard deviation, so the unit becomes "one standard deviation." Now $Z=2$ means "two standard deviations above the mean" in any dataset. This converts any normal distribution into the one standard normal with mean 0 and SD 1.

Each term, decoded
$x$
The raw value
The data point you're standardizing.
$\mu$
Mean
Center of the distribution.
find it given, or computed via F01
$\sigma$
Standard deviation
The "ruler" — one unit of spread.
find it given, or computed via F02
Try it

Score $x=80$, mean $\mu=50$, SD $\sigma=15$.

1 $Z=\frac{80-50}{15}=\frac{30}{15}=2$. The score is 2 SDs above average.

F10

Standard Error

How much a sample mean wobbles — and why bigger samples wobble less.

Standard Error of the Mean
$$SE=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Why we need it

You take a sample and compute its mean. Take a different sample — you'd get a slightly different mean. Sample means bounce around the true mean from sample to sample. Standard error measures how much that bounce is. It's the key ingredient for confidence intervals and every test about a mean.

Where it comes from

When you average $n$ values, the random ups and downs partly cancel each other out — one value being too high is offset by another being too low. More values → more cancellation → a steadier average. The math of how variance combines shows the wobble shrinks in proportion to $\sqrt{n}$, not $n$ (because variances add, and standard deviation is their square root). So to halve your error you need four times the data. That's why the $\sqrt{}$ is there.

Each term, decoded
$\sigma$
Population standard deviation
The spread of individual values.
find it given in the problem (or use sample SD $s$ if unknown)
$n$
Sample size
How many observations are in your sample.
$\sqrt{n}$
Square root of the sample size
The reason large samples give precise means.
Try it

$\sigma=12$, sample size $n=36$.

1 $SE=\frac{12}{\sqrt{36}}=\frac{12}{6}=2$.

F11

Confidence Interval

Turning a single sample mean into an honest range for the true mean.

Confidence Interval for the Mean
$$\bar{x}\ \pm\ Z\cdot\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Why we need it

Your sample mean is a guess for the true population mean, but it's almost never exactly right. Reporting just $\bar x$ pretends to a precision you don't have. A confidence interval is honest: it gives a range that very likely contains the true mean, along with how confident you are (e.g. 95%).

Where it comes from

Start from your best guess, the sample mean $\bar x$ (center of the interval). Now reach out on both sides by a margin of error. How far? Far enough to capture the true mean with your desired confidence. The margin is built from two pieces: the standard error $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$ (how much sample means wobble — F10) times a Z critical value that says how many standard errors wide to go for your confidence level (1.96 for 95%, because ~95% of a normal sits within 1.96 SDs). Multiply them, add and subtract from $\bar x$.

Each term, decoded
$\bar{x}$
Sample mean
The center of your interval — your point estimate.
find it average of your sample (F01)
$Z$
Critical value for the confidence level
How many standard errors to extend.
find it 90%→1.645, 95%→1.96, 99%→2.576
$\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$
Standard error
The wobble of the sample mean.
find it compute via F10
$\pm$
Plus and minus
Build the lower end (−) and upper end (+).
i
What "95% confident" really means: not "95% chance the true mean is in this interval." It means the method works 95% of the time — if you repeated the whole sampling process many times, 95% of the intervals you'd build would capture the true mean.
Try it

$\bar x=72$, $\sigma=12$, $n=36$, 95% confidence.

1 $SE=\frac{12}{6}=2$.

2 Margin $=1.96\times2=3.92$.

3 CI $=72\pm3.92=(68.08,\ 75.92)$.

F12

The Chi-Square Statistic

Measuring how far observed counts stray from what you expected.

Chi-Square
$$\chi^2=\sum\frac{(O-E)^2}{E}$$
Why we need it

You have category counts — how many customers chose each of 4 networks, how many dice rolls landed on each face. You expected a certain pattern (maybe "equal across all"). The real counts never match perfectly. The question: is the mismatch just random noise, or a real effect? Chi-square turns the total mismatch into one number you can test.

Where it comes from

For each category, look at the gap between Observed and Expected, $(O-E)$. Square it so over- and under-counts don't cancel and big gaps count more (same trick as variance). Then divide by E — this is the clever part: a gap of 10 is a big deal if you only expected 20, but trivial if you expected 10,000. Dividing by E scales the gap to its context. Finally, sum across all categories to get the total discrepancy. Big total → observed and expected are far apart → reject the assumption.

Each term, decoded
$O$
Observed count
What you actually counted in each category.
find it directly from the data table
$E$
Expected count
What you'd expect if the null hypothesis were true.
find it e.g. equal split = total ÷ number of categories
$\sum$
Sum over all categories
Add up each category's contribution.
i
Then compare: compute the degrees of freedom (categories − 1 for a goodness-of-fit test), look up the critical χ² value, and if your calculated χ² exceeds it, reject the null. Otherwise the mismatch is just noise.
Try it

500 users, 4 networks, expecting 125 each. Network A had 105.

1 A's term $=\frac{(105-125)^2}{125}=\frac{400}{125}=3.20$.

2 Repeat for B, C, D and add → that total is χ².

F13

The ANOVA F-Statistic

Comparing 3+ group means by pitting two kinds of variation against each other.

F-ratio
$$F=\frac{MSB}{MSW}=\frac{\text{variation between groups}}{\text{variation within groups}}$$
Why we need it

You tested 3 teaching methods and got 3 different average scores. But group averages always differ a little by chance. The question: are the methods genuinely different, or did the gaps appear just from random student-to-student variation? ANOVA answers this for any number of groups at once.

Where it comes from

Clever idea: compare two sources of variation.

Between-group variation (MSB): how far apart the group means are from the overall mean. If the methods truly differ, this is large.

Within-group variation (MSW): how much individuals scatter inside their own group. This is the natural "background noise" that exists no matter what.

Take their ratio. If between-group variation is much bigger than the background noise, $F$ is large → the group differences are real, not noise. If $F$ is near 1, the gaps between groups are no bigger than the random scatter inside them → no real difference. We divide the sums of squares by their degrees of freedom first (giving "mean squares") so the comparison is fair.

Each term, decoded
$MSB$
Mean Square Between
Between-group variation per degree of freedom.
find it $MSB=\dfrac{SSB}{k-1}$, where $SSB=\sum n_j(\bar x_j-\bar x)^2$
$MSW$
Mean Square Within
Within-group (noise) variation per degree of freedom.
find it $MSW=\dfrac{SSW}{N-k}$, where $SSW=\sum\sum(x-\bar x_j)^2$
$k$
Number of groups
How many groups you're comparing.
$N$
Total observations
All data points across every group.
$\bar x_j$
Each group's mean
vs $\bar x$, the grand mean of everything.
Try it

Three groups, means 12/16/20, grand mean 16, $n=3$ each.

1 $SSB=3[(12{-}16)^2+0+(20{-}16)^2]=3(32)=96$, so $MSB=96/2=48$.

2 If $SSW=24$ then $MSW=24/6=4$.

3 $F=48/4=12$ → far above 1 → groups differ.

F14

The Determinant (2×2)

One number that tells you if a matrix can be "undone."

2×2 Determinant
$$\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}=ad-bc$$
Why we need it

A matrix transforms space (stretches, rotates, shears it). The determinant tells you the area scaling factor of that transformation — and crucially, whether the transformation is reversible. If the determinant is 0, the matrix squashes 2D space flat onto a line, destroying information that can never be recovered → the matrix has no inverse (it's "singular"). This single number gates inverses, solving equations, and more.

Where it comes from

The columns of the matrix, $\binom{a}{c}$ and $\binom{b}{d}$, are two vectors. They define a parallelogram. The area of that parallelogram is $ad-bc$ (it's the cross-product area formula in 2D). If the two columns point in the same direction (one is a multiple of the other), the parallelogram is flat — zero area — and $ad-bc=0$. That flatness is exactly what "linearly dependent / singular / no inverse" means.

Each term, decoded
$a,d$
The main diagonal entries
Top-left and bottom-right. Their product $ad$ is the first piece.
$b,c$
The anti-diagonal entries
Top-right and bottom-left. Their product $bc$ gets subtracted.
i
Instant zero check: if one row is a multiple of the other (e.g. row 2 = 2 × row 1), the determinant is 0 — the columns are dependent, area is flat, no need to calculate.
Try it

$\begin{bmatrix}2&3\\1&4\end{bmatrix}$

1 $ad-bc=(2)(4)-(3)(1)=8-3=5$. Non-zero → invertible.

F15

Matrix Multiplication

Why it's "rows times columns," and why the shapes must match.

Entry of a Product
$$c_{ij}=\sum_{k} a_{ik}\,b_{kj}$$
Why we need it

Multiplying matrices chains two transformations into one, and it's how you apply a transformation to data, run a layer of a neural network, or combine systems of equations. It's the workhorse operation of all of linear algebra and machine learning.

Where it comes from

Each entry of the answer is a dot product: take row $i$ from the left matrix and column $j$ from the right matrix, multiply them element-by-element, and add. Why this pattern? Because that's exactly what "apply transformation B, then transformation A" works out to algebraically — each output coordinate is a weighted combination of the inputs, and those weights are a row dotted with a column.

This is also why the inner dimensions must match: a row of the left and a column of the right have to be the same length to pair up term-by-term. (m×n) · (n×p) works; the shared n is consumed, leaving an m×p result.

Each term, decoded
$c_{ij}$
Output entry, row i column j
The single number you're filling in.
$a_{ik}$
Row i of the left matrix
Walks across the row as k changes.
$b_{kj}$
Column j of the right matrix
Walks down the column as k changes.
$\sum_k$
Sum over the shared dimension
Pair up and add all the products.
!
Order matters: $AB\neq BA$ in general. "Rotate then stretch" is not the same as "stretch then rotate."
Try it

Top-left entry of $\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}5&6\\7&8\end{bmatrix}$:

1 Row 1 · Col 1 $=(1)(5)+(2)(7)=5+14=19$.

F16

Matrix Inverse (2×2)

The "undo" matrix — and why the swap-negate-divide recipe works.

2×2 Inverse
$$A^{-1}=\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{bmatrix}$$
Why we need it

The inverse reverses what a matrix does, and it's how you solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ for the unknown $\mathbf{x}$ — the matrix version of dividing. Just like $5x=10$ is solved by multiplying by $\frac{1}{5}$, $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ is solved by multiplying by $A^{-1}$.

Where it comes from

The inverse must satisfy $A\,A^{-1}=I$ (the identity, the "do-nothing" matrix). If you write out that requirement for a general 2×2 and solve the resulting equations, you get exactly this recipe: swap the diagonal entries (a↔d), negate the off-diagonal entries (b, c), and divide everything by the determinant. The division by $ad-bc$ is the same $\frac{1}{5}$ idea — and it instantly explains why a determinant of 0 means no inverse exists: you can't divide by zero.

$ad{-}bc$
The determinant
If it's 0, stop — no inverse exists.
find it compute via F14
$d,a$
Swapped diagonal
a and d trade places.
$-b,-c$
Negated off-diagonal
b and c keep their position but flip sign.
Try it

$A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\5&3\end{bmatrix}$

1 det $=2(3)-1(5)=1$.

2 Swap/negate: $\begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{bmatrix}$, divide by 1.

3 $A^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{bmatrix}$.

F17

The Dot Product

One number that captures how much two vectors point the same way.

Dot Product
$$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\sum_i a_ib_i=\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$$
Why we need it

It answers "how aligned are these two vectors?" — which underlies measuring similarity (recommender systems, search, NLP word embeddings), projecting one vector onto another, and testing for perpendicularity. It's arguably the most-used single operation in machine learning.

Where it comes from

There are two faces of the same thing. The computational side, $\sum a_ib_i$, just says "multiply matching components and add" — easy to calculate. The geometric side, $\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|\cos\theta$, reveals its meaning: it's the two lengths multiplied, scaled by $\cos\theta$ of the angle between them. When they point the same way ($\theta=0$, $\cos=1$) the dot product is large and positive; perpendicular ($\theta=90°$, $\cos=0$) gives zero; opposite directions give negative. So a dot product of 0 is the clean algebraic signal for "perpendicular."

Each term, decoded
$a_i,b_i$

F18

Cosine Similarity

Dot product, but stripped of length so only direction matters.

Cosine Similarity
$$\cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|}$$
Why we need it

Often we care about direction, not size. Two documents about the same topic should count as similar even if one is much longer (bigger vector). Plain dot product is inflated by length; cosine similarity removes that so you measure pure orientation — perfect for comparing texts, users, or any feature vectors.

Where it comes from

Rearrange the geometric dot product $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|\cos\theta$ to solve for $\cos\theta$: just divide both sides by the two lengths. Dividing by the lengths normalizes the vectors to unit size, so what's left is only the cosine of the angle — a clean score from −1 (opposite) through 0 (perpendicular) to +1 (identical direction), independent of how long the vectors are.

F19

Distance from a Point to a Plane

How far a data point sits from a decision boundary.

Point-to-Hyperplane Distance
$$d=\frac{|\mathbf{w}^{T}\mathbf{x}_0+b|}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}$$
Why we need it

In classification (e.g. a Support Vector Machine), a hyperplane is the boundary separating two classes. How confidently a point belongs to its class depends on how far it is from that boundary. This formula gives that distance — central to margins and classifier confidence.

Where it comes from

A plane is written $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x+b=0$, where $\mathbf w$ is the vector perpendicular to the plane (its "normal"). Plug your point $\mathbf x_0$ into the left side: $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x_0+b$. On the plane this is 0; off the plane it's non-zero, and its size grows with distance — but it's also inflated by how long $\mathbf w$ is. So you divide by $\|\mathbf w\|$ to cancel that scaling and get the true geometric distance. The absolute value just makes distance positive regardless of which side the point is on.

2 Bottom: $\|\mathbf w\|=\sqrt{9+16}=5$.

F20

The Eigenvalue Equation

Finding the special directions a matrix only stretches.

Eigen-equation & how to solve it
$$A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\ \ \Longrightarrow\ \ \det(A-\lambda I)=0$$
Why we need it

Most vectors get knocked off their direction when a matrix acts on them. A few special ones — the eigenvectors — keep their direction and only get scaled. Those directions reveal the "natural axes" of a transformation, and they power PCA (finding the directions of greatest variance in data), Google's PageRank, vibration analysis, and much more.

Where it comes from

We want a non-zero vector $\mathbf v$ that the matrix only stretches: $A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v$ (output is just $\lambda$ times the input). Rearrange: $A\mathbf v-\lambda\mathbf v=\mathbf 0$, then $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf v=\mathbf 0$ (the $I$ lets us subtract a scalar from a matrix properly). Now here's the key: this says the matrix $(A-\lambda I)$ squashes a non-zero $\mathbf v$ down to zero. A matrix can only do that if it's singular — i.e. its determinant is 0. So we solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ to find the $\lambda$ values, then plug each back in to find its $\mathbf v$.

Eigenvector
The direction that only gets scaled, not rotated.
find it solve $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf v=\mathbf 0$ for each λ
$I$
Identity matrix
Lets you subtract λ from the matrix on the diagonal.
!

Try it

$A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}$

1 $\det(A-\lambda I)=(4-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-2=\lambda^2-7\lambda+10$.

2 Solve $=0$: $(\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)=0\Rightarrow\lambda=5,2$.

3 Check: trace $7=5+2$ ✓, det $10=5\times2$ ✓.

F21

The Rank–Nullity Theorem

Every column is accounted for — either it adds a new direction, or it doesn't.

Rank–Nullity
$$\operatorname{rank}(A)+\operatorname{nullity}(A)=\text{number of columns}$$
Why we need it

It connects two ideas — how much "real information" a matrix carries (rank) and how much it "throws away" (nullity) — into one bookkeeping identity. It instantly tells you the size of the solution space of $A\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$ and whether a system has unique or infinitely many solutions.

Where it comes from

Think of each column as contributing one input dimension. When the matrix acts, each column either adds a genuinely new output direction (it counts toward the rank) or it's redundant — a combination of others — and collapses to zero, contributing a direction to the null space (counting toward nullity). Every column falls into exactly one bucket, so the two buckets must add back up to the total number of columns. It's conservation of dimensions.

Number of linearly independent columns (genuine directions).

Nullity of A
Dimension of the null space — solutions to $A\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$.
find it columns − rank (rearrange this very formula)

Number of columns
The total input dimensions of the matrix.
find it just count the columns

1 nullity $=5-3=2$. Its null space is 2-dimensional.
