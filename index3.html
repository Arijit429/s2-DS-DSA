Part I

Foundations & Descriptive Statistics

S01

What Statistics Is

The vocabulary every later topic quietly depends on.

The two branches
Descriptive — summarize the data you have.
Inferential — use a sample to conclude about a population.
Why we need it

Raw data is just a pile of numbers. Statistics is the discipline of turning that pile into understanding and into decisions made under uncertainty. Getting the basic vocabulary straight is what keeps the whole second half of the course (inference) from becoming confusing.

Where it comes from

Two complementary jobs. Descriptive statistics describes data you already hold — means, charts, standard deviations — making no claim beyond it. Inferential statistics uses a manageable sample to draw conclusions about a much larger population you can't measure fully (via confidence intervals and hypothesis tests).

Try it

You measure 200 of 50,000 students to estimate the average score.

→ The 200 are the sample; their average is a statistic; the true 50,000 average is the parameter you're estimating.

S02

Central Tendency

Three ways to describe the "center" — and when each one misleads.

Mean · Median · Mode
$$\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\qquad \text{median = middle value}\qquad \text{mode = most frequent}$$
Why we need it

You want a single number that represents the "typical" value of a dataset. But which one? The three measures answer that differently, and choosing wrong (mean on skewed data, say) gives a misleading picture.

Where it comes from

Mean — share the total equally among all points; it's the balance point, but every value (including outliers) tugs on it. Median — sort the data and take the positional middle; outliers can't drag it, so it's robust for skewed data. Mode — the value that occurs most often; the only one usable on categories.

  • If $n$ is odd, the median is the single middle value; if even, it's the average of the two middle values.

$\sum x$
Sum of all values
Add every data point.
$n$
Count
Number of data points.
!
Classic exam trap: "which measure is least affected by outliers?" → the median.
Try it

Data: {4, 5, 6, 7, 100}.

1 Mean $=\tfrac{122}{5}=24.4$ — dragged up by the outlier.

2 Median $=6$ — the honest center. This is why skewed data uses the median.

S03

Variability & Spread

Center isn't enough — you also need how spread out the data is.

Variance → Std Dev → CV
$$\sigma^2=\frac{\sum(x-\mu)^2}{N}\qquad \sigma=\sqrt{\sigma^2}\qquad CV=\frac{\sigma}{\bar x}\times100\%$$
Why we need it

Two classes can both average 70: one with everyone at 68–72, another split between 40 and 100. The mean can't tell them apart — you need a number for how far values typically sit from the mean.

Where it comes from

Measure each point's distance from the mean, $(x-\mu)$. These cancel to zero if you average them directly, so square them first — that kills negatives and punishes big gaps more. Averaging the squared distances gives the variance. Since variance is in squared units, take the square root to return to the original units → the standard deviation. The range (max − min) and MAD ($\tfrac{\sum|x-\bar x|}{n}$) are simpler alternatives; the CV divides σ by the mean to compare spreads across different scales.

Each term, decoded
$x-\mu$

Distance of one value from the mean.
find it subtract the mean from each value
$N$
Population size
Sample version divides by $n-1$ instead.
$CV$
Coefficient of variation
Spread as a % of the mean — unitless, comparable across datasets.
!
The n−1 rule: a sample variance divides by $n-1$ (not $N$). You spent one piece of information estimating the mean $\bar x$, so $n-1$ corrects for it. Whole group → N; sample → n−1.
Try it

Data {2, 4, 6} as a full population.

1 Mean $=4$; deviations $-2,0,+2$; squared $4,0,4$.

2 Variance $=\tfrac{8}{3}\approx2.67$; SD $=\sqrt{2.67}\approx1.63$.

S04

Quartiles, IQR & Outliers

A spread measure that ignores extremes, plus the rule for flagging them.

IQR & the outlier fences
$$IQR=Q_3-Q_1\qquad [\,Q_1-1.5\,IQR,\ \ Q_3+1.5\,IQR\,]$$
Why we need it

The range is wrecked by a single freak value. We want a spread measure built on the bulk of the data, plus an objective rule to decide what counts as an outlier instead of eyeballing it.

Where it comes from

Sort the data and cut it into four equal quarters; the cut points are $Q_1$ (25%), $Q_2$ (median), $Q_3$ (75%). The IQR is the width of the middle 50% — immune to extremes. For outliers, step 1.5 IQRs beyond $Q_1$ and $Q_3$ to build "fences"; anything past them is unusually far from the bulk and gets flagged. (A percentile $P_k$ is the value below which $k\%$ of data falls; deciles split into ten.)

$1.5$

Try it

$Q_1=20$, $Q_3=40$. Is 75 an outlier?

1 $IQR=20$; upper fence $=40+1.5(20)=70$.

2 $75>70$ → yes, an outlier.

S05

Distribution Shape

Skewness (asymmetry) and kurtosis (tailedness).

Two shape descriptors
Skewness — which way the tail points.
Kurtosis — how heavy the tails are.
Why we need it

Mean and spread don't capture shape. Skewness and kurtosis tell you whether the data leans one way and whether it has fat tails (more extreme values than a normal would) — both crucial for choosing the right model and test.

Where it comes from

Skewness measures asymmetry. The mean gets "pulled toward the tail," so a long right tail makes mean > median (right/positive skew); a long left tail makes mean < median. Kurtosis measures peak/tail heaviness relative to a normal: leptokurtic (>3) = sharp peak, heavy tails; mesokurtic (=3) = normal; platykurtic (<3) = flat, thin tails.

Try it

Income data: a few very high earners stretch the right tail.

→ Right-skewed, so mean > median — which is why median income is usually reported.

Part II

Random Variables

S07

Random Variables (PMF / PDF / CDF)

Turning random outcomes into numbers we can do math on.

Discrete vs Continuous
$$\text{PMF}: P(X{=}x)\quad\bigg|\quad \text{PDF}: f(x)\quad\bigg|\quad \text{CDF}: F(x)=P(X\le x)$$
Why we need it

To reason about uncertainty with math, we attach numbers to random outcomes. A random variable is a function assigning a number to each outcome of an experiment — the bridge from "events" to algebra.

Where it comes from
  • Discrete RV — countable values (heads count, dice). Described by a PMF giving $P(X=x)$ for each value; all probabilities sum to 1.
  • Continuous RV — any value in a range (height, time). Described by a PDF $f(x)$; the area under it over an interval is the probability, and the total area is 1.
  • The CDF $F(x)=P(X\le x)$ accumulates probability up to $x$ — it works for both types.
!
Continuous subtlety: for a continuous RV, $P(X=\text{an exact value})=0$. Only intervals carry probability; the PDF height is a density, not a probability.
Try it

A fair die is a discrete RV.

→ PMF: $P(X=x)=\tfrac16$ for $x=1,\dots,6$; CDF: $F(3)=P(X\le3)=\tfrac36=0.5$.

S08

Expectation E(X)

The "mean" of a random variable — the long-run average outcome.

Expected Value & properties
$$E(X)=\sum x\,P(x)\qquad E(aX+b)=a\,E(X)+b$$
Why we need it

When outcomes are random (a bet, an insurance payout), you want to know the average result over many repeats. That long-run average is the expectation — the basis for deciding whether something is worth doing.

Where it comes from

An ordinary average weights all values equally, but random outcomes aren't equally likely. So we take a weighted average: each value is weighted by its probability. Frequent outcomes pull the average toward themselves; rare ones barely move it. Useful properties: $E(X+Y)=E(X)+E(Y)$ always, and $E(XY)=E(X)E(Y)$ only when independent.

A possible outcome

$P(x)$

find it from the problem's probability table
Try it

Pays ₹0 (prob 0.5), ₹10 (0.3), ₹100 (0.2).

1 $E(X)=0(0.5)+10(0.3)+100(0.2)=23$ → ₹23 average per play.

S09

Variance of a Random Variable

The shortcut formula — the squared-deviation idea, rearranged.

Variance — computational form
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-\big[E(X)\big]^2\qquad \operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$$
Why we need it

The expected value doesn't tell you how risky / spread out random outcomes are. A lottery and a savings account can share an expected value but differ wildly in risk — variance captures that.

Where it comes from

By definition variance is $E[(X-\mu)^2]$ — the expected squared deviation. Expanding that bracket and simplifying collapses to the easy-to-compute form $E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2$: "the mean of the squares minus the square of the mean." Note the shift rule: adding a constant $b$ doesn't change spread, so $\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$.

Each term, decoded
$E(X^2)$
Mean of X-squared
Square each outcome, then average.
find it $\sum x^2 P(x)$
$[E(X)]^2$
Mean, then squared
Average first, then square the single number.
!
Don't swap these: $E(X^2)$ = square then average; $[E(X)]^2$ = average then square. Their difference is the variance.
Try it

Given $E(X)=3$, $E(X^2)=13$.

1 $\operatorname{Var}(X)=13-3^2=4$.

Part III

Probability Distributions

S10

Bernoulli Distribution

The simplest random experiment: one trial, success or failure.

Bernoulli(p)
$$P(X{=}1)=p,\quad P(X{=}0)=1-p\qquad \text{Mean}=p,\quad \operatorname{Var}=p(1-p)$$
Why we need it

It models any single yes/no event — a coin flip, a click, a pass/fail. It's also the atom that the Binomial is built from, so understanding it unlocks the next one.

Where it comes from

Define success as 1 (probability $p$) and failure as 0 (probability $1-p$). The mean is $E(X)=1\cdot p+0\cdot(1-p)=p$. The variance works out to $p(1-p)$, which is largest at $p=0.5$ (a 50/50 event is the most unpredictable) and shrinks to 0 as $p$ approaches 0 or 1 (near-certain events have little variability).

$p$
Probability of success
The chance the trial "works."
find it given in the problem
$1{-}p$
Probability of failure
Whatever's left over.
Try it

$X\sim$ Bernoulli(0.7).

1 Mean $=0.7$; Variance $=0.7\times0.3=0.21$.

S11

Binomial Distribution

Counting successes in n independent yes/no trials.

Binomial(n, p)
$$P(X{=}k)=\binom{n}{k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}\qquad \text{Mean}=np,\quad \operatorname{Var}=np(1-p)$$
Why we need it

Endless situations are "repeat a yes/no trial $n$ times": flip a coin 10 times, test 20 products, treat 5 patients. We want the probability of exactly $k$ successes — and counting by hand explodes, so the formula does it directly.

Where it comes from

Three questions. (1) One specific pattern of $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures has probability $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ (independent trials multiply). (2) The successes can fall in many orders, and the number of arrangements is the combination $\binom{n}{k}$. (3) Add up all those equally-likely patterns: multiply the count by the per-pattern probability.

Each term, decoded
$n$
Number of trials
How many repeats.
$k$
Successes you want
The exact count asked about.
$\binom{n}{k}$
"n choose k"
Number of ways to place k successes among n trials.
find it $\dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$
Try it

Fair coin, 4 tosses, P(exactly 2 heads). $n=4,k=2,p=0.5$.

1 $\binom42=6$; $(0.5)^2(0.5)^2=0.0625$.

2 $P=6\times0.0625=0.375=\tfrac38$.

S12

Poisson Distribution

Counting rare events in a fixed window of time or space.

Poisson(λ)
$$P(X{=}k)=\frac{\lambda^{k}e^{-\lambda}}{k!}\qquad \boxed{\text{Mean}=\operatorname{Var}=\lambda}$$
Why we need it

Some things happen at a steady average rate but at random moments — calls per hour, breakdowns per week, typos per page. There's no fixed "$n$ trials"; events just arrive. The Poisson gives the probability of seeing exactly $k$ in one window.

Where it comes from

Slice the window into a huge number of tiny instants, each with a tiny chance of an event — that's secretly a binomial with enormous $n$ and tiny $p$. Take the binomial limit as $n\to\infty$ with the average count $np=\lambda$ held fixed, and it simplifies into the Poisson formula. The constant $e$ falls out of that limit; the $k!$ is the leftover of the combination term.

$e$

$k!$

$k(k-1)\cdots1$; and $0!=1$.
!
Signature fact: for Poisson the mean and variance are both λ. Asked constantly.
Try it

Breakdowns average λ=2/week. P(exactly 1)?

1 $P(1)=\tfrac{2^1 e^{-2}}{1!}=2e^{-2}\approx2(0.135)=0.271$.

S13

Discrete Uniform Distribution

When every outcome in a set is equally likely.

PMF · Mean · Variance
$$P(X{=}x)=\frac{1}{N}\qquad \mu=\frac{a+b}{2}\qquad \sigma^2=\frac{N^2-1}{12}$$
Why we need it

The simplest distribution: a fair die, a random integer between two bounds. Whenever nothing makes one outcome likelier than another, this is the model — and it's the baseline for a chi-square goodness-of-fit test.

Where it comes from

PMF: $N$ equally likely values split total probability 1 evenly → $\tfrac1N$ each. Mean: for evenly spaced values the balance point is the midpoint $\tfrac{a+b}{2}$. Variance: working out $E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2$ for consecutive integers gives the tidy $\tfrac{N^2-1}{12}$ — spread grows with $N$, and 12 is the constant that drops out.

Each term, decoded
$N$
Number of values
Count of equally likely outcomes.
find it $N=b-a+1$
$a,b$

The bounds of the range.
Try it

Fair die: $a=1,b=6,N=6$.

1 Each face $\tfrac16$; Mean $=3.5$; Variance $=\tfrac{35}{12}\approx2.92$.

S14

Exponential Distribution

The waiting time between random events arriving at a steady rate.

PDF · CDF · Mean · Variance
$$f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\quad P(X\le x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}\quad \mu=\frac1\lambda\quad \sigma^2=\frac1{\lambda^2}$$
Why we need it

If events happen at a constant rate (the Poisson setup), the time you wait until the next one is exponential. It's the standard model for lifetimes, service times, and time-to-failure.

Where it comes from

Mean $=\tfrac1\lambda$: at rate $\lambda$ per hour, the average gap is the reciprocal. CDF: accumulating the PDF from 0 to $x$ gives $1-e^{-\lambda x}$, so $e^{-\lambda x}$ is the chance of still waiting after time $x$. This decaying form produces the famous memoryless property — past waiting tells you nothing about future waiting.

Try it

Buses at λ=0.5/min. P(wait ≤ 3 min)?

1 $1-e^{-0.5(3)}=1-e^{-1.5}\approx1-0.223=0.777$. Mean wait $=1/0.5=2$ min.

S15

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

The bell curve — and what the intimidating PDF actually says.

Normal PDF
$$f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$$
Why we need it

The most important distribution in statistics. Heights, errors, and (via the CLT) sample means follow it. Z-scores, confidence intervals, and most tests assume normality, so this curve underpins all of inference.

Where it comes from

The exponent $-\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2$: the inside is the z-score — distance from the mean in SD units. Squaring makes the curve symmetric; the minus sign makes probability decay away from $\mu$ → the bell shape. The front factor $\tfrac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}$ is a normalizing constant that scales the total area to exactly 1.

i
Empirical Rule (normal data only): ≈68% within ±1σ, ≈95% within ±2σ, ≈99.7% within ±3σ. You rarely plug into the PDF by hand — you convert to a z-score and read a table.
Try it

1 $z=\tfrac{130-100}{15}=2$ — two SDs above; only ~2.5% score higher.

S16

The Z-Score

How many standard deviations a value sits from the mean.

Standardization
$$Z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$$
Why we need it

"You scored 80" means nothing alone — 80 with a class mean of 50 is brilliant; with a mean of 95 it's poor. The z-score expresses a value by how unusual it is on a universal scale, letting you compare scores from completely different tests.

Where it comes from

Two steps: (1) re-center by subtracting the mean, $x-\mu$, so 0 = average; (2) re-scale by dividing by the SD, so the unit becomes "one standard deviation." Now $Z=2$ means "two SDs above the mean" in any dataset — it converts any normal into the standard normal $N(0,1)$.

$x$
The raw value
The data point being standardized.
$\mu$

$\sigma$

Try it

1 $Z=\tfrac{80-50}{15}=2$ — the score is 2 SDs above average.

S17

Log-Normal Distribution

For positive, right-skewed data — where the logarithm is normal.

Log-Normal PDF
$$f(x)=\frac{1}{x\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(\ln x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right),\quad x>0$$
Why we need it

Many real quantities can't be negative and lean right: incomes, stock prices, file sizes. A normal fits badly (it allows negatives, is symmetric). The log-normal is built for positive, right-skewed data.

Where it comes from

The defining idea: $X$ is log-normal if $\ln(X)$ is normal. So you take the normal PDF and replace $x$ with $\ln x$ — hence $\ln x-\mu$ in the exponent. The extra $\tfrac1x$ is the correction that always appears when transforming a variable through the log (it keeps total area 1). Because the log compresses large values, the original $X$ comes out stretched into a positive, right-skewed shape.

S18

Chebyshev's Theorem — In Detail

A guarantee about spread that holds for every distribution, no matter its shape.

Chebyshev's Inequality — two equivalent forms
$$\underbrace{P\big(|X-\mu|
Why we need it

The empirical rule (68-95-99.7) only works if the data is normal. But very often you don't know the shape, or it clearly isn't a bell curve. Chebyshev's theorem still gives you a guaranteed minimum percentage of data within $k$ standard deviations of the mean — for any distribution whatsoever. That universality is the whole point: it's the safety net for when you can't assume normality.

Where it comes from — the actual derivation

It follows directly from the definition of variance. Variance is the average squared distance from the mean:

$$\sigma^2=\sum (x-\mu)^2\,P(x)$$

Step 1 — split the data into two groups: points near the mean (within $k\sigma$) and points far from it (at least $k\sigma$ away). The total is just the two pieces added:

$$\sigma^2=\underbrace{\sum_{|x-\mu|Step 2 — throw away the "near" group. It's a sum of non-negative things, so dropping it can only make the right side smaller — turning the equation into an inequality:

$$\sigma^2\ \ge\ \sum_{|x-\mu|\ge k\sigma}(x-\mu)^2P(x)$$

Step 3 — use that the far points are genuinely far. Each is at least $k\sigma$ from the mean, so $(x-\mu)^2\ge(k\sigma)^2$. Replace and pull the constant out:

$$\sigma^2\ \ge\ (k\sigma)^2\!\!\sum_{|x-\mu|\ge k\sigma}\!\!P(x)\ =\ k^2\sigma^2\cdot P\big(|X-\mu|\ge k\sigma\big)$$

Step 4 — divide both sides by $k^2\sigma^2$. The $\sigma^2$ cancels and the theorem drops out:

$$\frac{1}{k^2}\ \ge\ P\big(|X-\mu|\ge k\sigma\big)$$

That's the "beyond" form; subtracting from 1 gives the "within" form. The proof used no assumption about shape — only the variance — which is why it works everywhere, and also why it's loose (the real percentage is usually much higher).

i

• "At least what % lies in [a, b]?" → find $k=\dfrac{b-\mu}{\sigma}$ (interval symmetric about $\mu$), answer $1-\tfrac{1}{k^2}$.
• "Between which two values do at least P% lie?" → solve $1-\tfrac{1}{k^2}=P$ for $k$, interval is $\big(\mu-k\sigma,\ \mu+k\sigma\big)$.

Worked example — bolt lengths (distribution unknown)

A factory's bolts have mean length $\mu=50$ mm and standard deviation $\sigma=2$ mm. The shape is not known, so the empirical rule can't be used — but Chebyshev always can.

(a) At least what percentage of bolts are between 44 mm and 56 mm?

1 Symmetric about 50; distance to an endpoint $=56-50=6$ mm.

2 $k=\tfrac{6}{2}=3$.

3 $1-\tfrac{1}{3^2}=1-\tfrac19=0.889$.

→ At least 88.9% lie between 44 and 56 mm — regardless of shape.

(b) Between which two lengths are at least 75% guaranteed to fall?

1 $1-\tfrac{1}{k^2}=0.75\Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{k^2}=0.25\Rightarrow k=2$.

2 Interval $=\mu\pm k\sigma=50\pm2(2)=50\pm4$.

→ At least 75% lie between 46 mm and 54 mm.

(c) At most what percentage differ from the mean by 8 mm or more?

1 $k=\tfrac{8}{2}=4$.

2 Beyond form: $\tfrac{1}{k^2}=\tfrac{1}{16}=0.0625$.

→ At most 6.25% are 8 mm or more from 50 (outside 42–58 mm).

S19

QQ Plots (Quantile–Quantile)

A visual test for "does my data follow this distribution?"

What you plot
pairs of percentiles $(x_i,\ y_i)$ — theoretical vs your data's quantile.
A straight line ⟹ same distribution type.
Why we need it

Before using methods that assume normality (z-tests, CIs, ANOVA), check whether the data actually is roughly normal. A QQ plot is the fast visual way — and reveals skew or heavy tails at a glance.

Where it comes from

A quantile is a value below which a given fraction of data falls. Line up your data's quantiles against the quantiles a reference distribution (usually normal) would produce, plotting them as points $(x_i,y_i)$. If both are the same type, the quantiles rise together → points fall on a straight diagonal. Curves away from the line reveal exactly how the data differs (bending ends = heavy tails, C-shape = skew).

Each term, decoded
$x_i$
Theoretical quantile

S20

Box-Cox Transformation

A tunable "dial" that reshapes skewed data toward normal.

Box-Cox power transform
$$y_i=\begin{cases}\dfrac{x_i^{\lambda}-1}{\lambda}, & \lambda\neq0\\[8pt]\ln(x_i), & \lambda=0\end{cases}$$
Why we need it

Many methods need normal data, but real data is often skewed. Box-Cox gives a single parameter $\lambda$ you tune to transform non-Gaussian data into something close to normal — unlocking the tools that assume normality.

Where it comes from

It's a family of power transforms with one knob $\lambda$: $\lambda=2$ squares, $\lambda=\tfrac12$ square-roots, $\lambda=-1$ takes a reciprocal. You search for the $\lambda$ that makes the result most symmetric. The clever part: as $\lambda\to0$, $\tfrac{x^\lambda-1}{\lambda}$ smoothly approaches $\ln(x)$ — so the log transform is the natural limiting case and the whole family connects seamlessly.

Right-skewed income, trying $\lambda=0$ (log case).

Part V

Inferential Foundations

S21

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Why sample means are normal even when the data isn't.

CLT — the key results
$$\mu_{\bar{x}}=\mu\qquad \sigma^2_{\bar{x}}=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\qquad (n\ge30\Rightarrow \bar{x}\ \text{approx. normal})$$
Why we need it

It's the foundation that makes inference possible. The CLT is why we can build confidence intervals and run z-tests on a mean even with no idea of the population's shape.

Where it comes from

Take many samples and compute each mean. As $n$ grows: the averages center on the truth ($\mu_{\bar x}=\mu$); they tighten up because averaging cancels random highs and lows, so their variance shrinks to $\tfrac{\sigma^2}{n}$ (square root → standard error $\tfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$); and their shape becomes a normal bell curve once $n\ge30$, regardless of the population's shape. That convergence to normality is the theorem.

Each term, decoded
$\mu_{\bar x}$
Mean of the sample means
Equals the population mean μ.
$\sigma^2_{\bar x}$
Variance of the sample mean
$\sigma^2/n$ — shrinks as n grows.
find it divide population variance by n
Try it

$\mu=50,\sigma=12,n=36$.

1 Sample means center at 50; variance $=\tfrac{144}{36}=4$, so SE $=2$.

S22

Standard Error

How much a sample mean wobbles — and why bigger samples wobble less.

Standard Error of the Mean
$$SE=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Why we need it

Take one sample, get a mean; take another, get a slightly different one. Sample means bounce around the true mean. Standard error measures that bounce — the key ingredient for confidence intervals and every test about a mean.

Where it comes from

Averaging $n$ values makes the random ups and downs partly cancel — one value too high is offset by another too low. More values → more cancellation → steadier average. The math (variances add, then square-root) shows the wobble shrinks in proportion to $\sqrt n$, not $n$ — so to halve your error you need four times the data. That's why the square root is there.

Each term, decoded
$\sigma$
Population SD
Spread of individual values (use sample SD $s$ if unknown).
$\sqrt n$
Root of the sample size
Why large samples give precise means.
Try it

$\sigma=12$, $n=36$.

1 $SE=\tfrac{12}{\sqrt{36}}=\tfrac{12}{6}=2$.

S23

Confidence Interval for the Mean

A range for the true mean — using t when σ is unknown.

CI & Margin of Error
$$(\bar{x}-E,\ \bar{x}+E)\qquad E=t_{\alpha/2}\cdot\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Why we need it

A single sample mean is almost never exactly right. A CI reports an honest range likely to contain the true mean, with a stated confidence. When σ is unknown (the usual case), we use the t-distribution.

Where it comes from

Start at $\bar x$ and reach out by a margin $E$ each side. The margin = a critical value $t_{\alpha/2}$ (how wide for your confidence level) times the standard error $\tfrac{s}{\sqrt n}$ (using the sample SD $s$). We use $t$ rather than $Z$ because estimating σ with $s$ adds uncertainty — the t-distribution has slightly heavier tails to account for it, especially at small $n$; as $n$ grows, $t\to Z$.

$s$

Stands in for the unknown σ.
i
"95% confident" means the method captures the true mean 95% of the time across repeated samples — not "95% chance μ is in this one interval."
Try it

$n=25,\bar x=100,s=10$, 95% CI (t at df 24 ≈ 2.064).

1 SE $=\tfrac{10}{5}=2$; $E=2.064\times2=4.13$.

2 CI $=100\pm4.13=(95.87,\ 104.13)$.

S24

Confidence Interval for Proportions

A range for a true percentage — polls, defect rates, click rates.

CI for a Proportion
$$\hat{p}\ \pm\ Z_c\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\,\hat{q}}{n}}$$
Why we need it

Often the quantity is a proportion, not a mean: voter support, defect rate, click-through rate. This interval gives a range for the true proportion — the math behind a poll's "margin of error."

Where it comes from

Same skeleton: estimate ± (critical value × standard error). The estimate is $\hat p$. The standard error of a proportion is $\sqrt{\tfrac{\hat p\hat q}{n}}$ (from the Bernoulli variance $p(1-p)$, divided by $n$, square-rooted). Multiply by the Z critical value and add/subtract from $\hat p$.

S25

Confidence Interval for Variance

A range for the true variability — built on the chi-square distribution.

CI for σ²
$$\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2}}\ <\ \sigma^2\ <\ \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}}$$
Why we need it

Sometimes the spread itself is what matters — the consistency of a process, the volatility of returns. This interval estimates the true population variance $\sigma^2$ from a sample.

Where it comes from

Key fact: $\tfrac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2}$ follows a chi-square distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom. Because chi-square is not symmetric, there's no simple "± margin." Instead grab the two cutoffs leaving $\tfrac{\alpha}{2}$ in each tail, then rearrange to bracket $\sigma^2$. The larger cutoff ends up under the lower bound, which is why the subscripts look flipped.

Part VI

Hypothesis Testing Framework

S26

Null & Alternative Hypotheses

The two competing claims every test is built around.

The two hypotheses
$$H_0:\ \mu=57\qquad\text{vs}\qquad H_1:\ \mu\neq57$$
Why we need it

A hypothesis test is a structured way to choose between two stories using data. You state them precisely before testing, so the test has a clear question and the result can't be fudged afterward.

Where it comes from

$H_0$ (null) is the status quo — "nothing unusual," assumed true until data forces otherwise (e.g. "mean is 57"). $H_1$ (alternative) is the research claim you're trying to demonstrate (e.g. "mean is not 57"). Like a courtroom: $H_0$ is "innocent" (presumed); strong evidence is needed to reject it. You never "prove $H_0$" — you only fail to reject it.

Each term, decoded
$H_0$
Null hypothesis
Status-quo claim; always contains "=".
$H_1$
Alternative hypothesis
The research claim ($\neq$, >, or <).
i
One- vs two-tailed: $H_1:\mu\neq57$ is two-tailed; $H_1:\mu>57$ or $<57$ is one-tailed. The form of $H_1$ decides which tails you test.
Try it

Claim: a new method changes the average score from 57.

→ $H_0:\mu=57$ (no change); $H_1:\mu\neq57$ (it changes the mean).

S27

The p-value

How surprising your data would be if the null were true.

Decision rule
$$\text{p-value}\le\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{reject } H_0$$
Why we need it

The p-value turns your test statistic into a single interpretable probability that decides whether to reject $H_0$. It's reported by virtually every test.

Where it comes from

Assume $H_0$ is true, then ask: how likely is data at least as extreme as what I observed? That probability is the p-value. Small → your data would be a big coincidence under $H_0$, so reject it. Large → consistent with $H_0$, so don't. Compare to a pre-set threshold $\alpha$ (usually 0.05).

find it from the test statistic via a Z/t/χ² table
$\alpha$

Your threshold (0.05) — also the Type I error rate.
!
Common misreading: the p-value is not "the probability that $H_0$ is true." It's the probability of the data given $H_0$.

1 $0.03\le0.05$ → reject $H_0$; statistically significant.

S28

One-Sample T-Test Statistic

Testing a mean when the sample is small and σ is unknown.

t statistic
$$t_{cal}=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}}$$
Why we need it

You want to test a claim about a mean, but with a small sample and unknown σ — the realistic case. The t-test handles exactly this by standardizing how far your sample mean is from the claimed value.

Where it comes from

It's a signal-to-noise ratio. The top $\bar x-\mu$ is the signal: how far your mean is from the $H_0$ value. The bottom $\tfrac{s}{\sqrt n}$ is the noise: the standard error. Dividing tells you how many standard errors the gap is — a big $t$ means strong evidence against $H_0$. It uses the sample SD $s$, so it's compared against the t-distribution with $n-1$ df.

i
Z-test vs T-test: if σ is known (or n is large), the same idea uses $Z=\tfrac{\bar x-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}$ and the normal table instead. Use t when σ is unknown and n is small.
Try it

$H_0:\mu=50$. Sample $\bar x=53,s=6,n=16$.

S29

Two-Sample T-Test (Pooled Variance)

Comparing two group means when their variances are assumed equal.

Pooled variance & the t statistic
$$s_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}\qquad t=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}}}$$
Why we need it

Constantly you compare two groups: treatment vs control, method A vs B, before vs after. This test decides whether the difference between means is real or just noise. When both groups share a variance, the pooled version is the right tool.

Where it comes from

Pooled variance: if both groups truly share one variance, combine both samples' information into one best estimate rather than use either alone — a weighted average of the two sample variances, each weighted by its df ($n-1$). The t statistic: same signal-to-noise idea — the top is the difference between group means, the bottom is the standard error of that difference, built from the pooled variance across both sample sizes.

Try it

1 $s_p^2=\tfrac{9(4)+11(6)}{20}=\tfrac{102}{20}=5.1$; use $s_p\approx2.26$ with the two means.

S30

The Chi-Square Test

Measuring how far observed counts stray from what you expected.

Chi-Square statistic
$$\chi^2=\sum\frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\qquad \text{df}=k-1\ \text{(fit)}$$
Why we need it

You have category counts — customers per network, dice rolls per face. You expected a pattern; the real counts never match perfectly. The question: is the mismatch random noise or a real effect? Chi-square turns the total mismatch into one testable number, for goodness-of-fit and for independence between categorical variables.

Where it comes from

For each category, take the gap $(O-E)$. Square it so over/under-counts don't cancel and big gaps count more. Then divide by $E$ — a gap of 10 is huge if you expected 20 but trivial if you expected 10,000; dividing by $E$ scales the gap to its context. Sum across categories for the total discrepancy. Big total → far from expected → reject $H_0$.

find it e.g. equal split = total ÷ categories

$k-1$ (fit) or $(r-1)(c-1)$ (independence).
Try it — are 4 networks equally used?

500 users: A=105, B=140, C=130, D=125; expected 125 each.

1 $\chi^2=\tfrac{20^2}{125}+\tfrac{15^2}{125}+\tfrac{5^2}{125}+\tfrac{0}{125}=3.2+1.8+0.2+0=5.2$.

2 df $=3$, critical $\chi^2_{0.05,3}=7.815$. Since $5.2<7.815$ → fail to reject; consistent with equal use.

S31

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

Comparing 3+ group means by pitting two kinds of variation against each other.

F-ratio
$$F=\frac{MSB}{MSW}=\frac{\text{variation between groups}}{\text{variation within groups}}$$
Why we need it

You tested 3 teaching methods and got 3 different averages. But group averages always differ a little by chance. The question: are the methods genuinely different, or did the gaps appear from random scatter? ANOVA answers this for any number of groups at once.

Where it comes from

Compare two variations. Between-group (MSB): how far apart the group means are from the overall mean — large if methods truly differ. Within-group (MSW): how much individuals scatter inside their own group — the background noise. Take the ratio: if between-group variation dwarfs the noise, $F$ is large → real differences; if $F\approx1$, the gaps are no bigger than random scatter → no difference. We divide the sums of squares by their df first (giving mean squares) so the comparison is fair.

Each term, decoded
$MSB$
Mean Square Between
Between-group variation per df.
find it $\tfrac{SSB}{k-1}$, $SSB=\sum n_j(\bar x_j-\bar x)^2$
$MSW$
Mean Square Within
Within-group (noise) variation per df.
find it $\tfrac{SSW}{N-k}$, $SSW=\sum\sum(x-\bar x_j)^2$
$k,N$
Groups & total observations
df: between $=k-1$, within $=N-k$.
Try it

1 $SSB=3[(12{-}16)^2+0+(20{-}16)^2]=96$, $MSB=48$.

2 Within each: (−2,0,2)→8 each → $SSW=24$, $MSW=4$.

3 $F=\tfrac{48}{4}=12>5.14$ → reject $H_0$; the methods differ.
