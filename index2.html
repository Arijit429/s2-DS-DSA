Part I

Determinants & Matrix Inverses

L01

The Determinant as a Scaling Factor

What that single number actually means geometrically.

2×2 Determinant
$$\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}=ad-bc$$
Why we need it

A matrix doesn't just hold numbers — it transforms space (stretching, rotating, shearing it). The determinant tells you the area scaling factor of that transformation in 2D (volume in 3D). A determinant of 2 means the transformation doubles areas; 0.5 means it halves them. Most importantly, a determinant of 0 means space has been squashed into a lower dimension — a 2D plane crushed onto a line — which destroys information permanently.

Where it comes from

The two columns of the matrix are vectors. They span a parallelogram, and the area of that parallelogram is exactly $ad-bc$. If the two columns point along the same line (one is a multiple of the other), the parallelogram collapses to a flat sliver of zero area — and $ad-bc=0$. That collapse is the geometric meaning of "linearly dependent," "singular," and "no inverse," all at once.

Each term, decoded
$a,d$
Main-diagonal entries
Top-left and bottom-right; their product is the first piece.
$b,c$
Anti-diagonal entries
Top-right and bottom-left; their product is subtracted.
!
The big takeaway: $\det=0 \Leftrightarrow$ space is squashed flat $\Leftrightarrow$ the matrix is singular $\Leftrightarrow$ no inverse exists. One number, four consequences.
Try it

$\begin{bmatrix}2&3\\1&4\end{bmatrix}$ vs $\begin{bmatrix}2&4\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$.

1 First: $2(4)-3(1)=5$ → scales area ×5, invertible.

2 Second: $2(2)-4(1)=0$ → squashed flat, singular (column 2 = 2× column 1).

L02

Computational Complexity of Determinants

Why the "obvious" method is unusable, and why properties matter.

Cost of expansion by minors
$$\text{operations}\ \approx\ n!\qquad 10!=3{,}628{,}800$$
Why we need it

Determinants are computed constantly in AI and engineering. But the textbook method — expansion by minors (cofactor expansion) — becomes catastrophically slow as the matrix grows. Knowing why tells you to never use it on large matrices, and to reach for determinant properties instead.

Where it comes from

Expansion by minors breaks an $n\times n$ determinant into $n$ smaller $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ determinants, each of which breaks into $(n-1)$ even smaller ones, and so on. That branching multiplies out to roughly $n!$ (n-factorial) operations. For a modest $10\times10$ matrix that's $10!=3.6$ million calculations; for $20\times20$ it exceeds the number of seconds since the Big Bang. The factorial growth is the killer.

The fix: use properties of determinants — especially that row-reduction to a triangular form lets you just multiply the diagonal. That approach costs about $n^3$ operations instead of $n!$ — for $n=10$, that's ~1,000 vs 3.6 million.

L03

The Identity Matrix

The matrix that does nothing — and why that's useful.

Identity & its determinant
$$I=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix},\qquad \det(I)=1$$
Why we need it

The identity matrix $I$ is the matrix equivalent of the number 1: multiplying any matrix by $I$ leaves it unchanged ($AI=A$). It's the "do-nothing" transformation, and it's the target you aim for when finding an inverse (since $A A^{-1}=I$) and the reference point in the eigenvalue equation.

Where it comes from

$I$ has 1s on the diagonal and 0s everywhere else, so when it transforms space, every axis maps to itself — nothing moves, nothing stretches. Since it doesn't change any areas or volumes, its scaling factor is 1 → $\det(I)=1$. The determinant of any identity matrix, of any size, is always 1.

L04

The Matrix Inverse

The "undo" matrix, and the two conditions it requires.

Definition of the inverse
$$AB=I\ \Longrightarrow\ B=A^{-1}$$
Why we need it

The inverse reverses a transformation and is how we solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ — the matrix version of dividing. Just as $5x=10$ is solved by multiplying by $\tfrac15$, the system $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ is solved by multiplying both sides by $A^{-1}$ to get $\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$.

Where it comes from

If a matrix $A$ does something to space, its inverse $B$ is the matrix that perfectly undoes it — do $A$ then $B$ and you're back where you started, i.e. $AB=I$. That's the entire definition.

Two conditions must hold:

• Square — input and output dimensions must match, or you can't map back exactly.

• Determinant ≠ 0 — if $\det A=0$ the transformation squashed space flat (L01), permanently losing information, so there's no way to reverse it. (And the inverse formula divides by $\det A$ — you can't divide by zero.)

!

L05

The Path to the Inverse

Three stepping stones: minors → cofactors → adjoint → inverse.

The full pipeline
$$A^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det A}\,\text{adj}(A)$$
Why we need it

For a general matrix (especially 3×3 and up) there's no one-line shortcut for the inverse. Instead you build it through three intermediate objects. Knowing the chain means you can invert any invertible matrix, not just memorize the 2×2 trick.

Where it comes from

Work through it as a relay of four stages:

1 · Minors. For each entry, delete its row and column and take the determinant of what remains. That's the minor $M_{ij}$ — a "sub-determinant."

2 · Cofactors. Attach a checkerboard of signs: $C_{ij}=(-1)^{i+j}M_{ij}$. Some minors flip sign; the pattern is $+\,-\,+\,\dots$

3 · Adjoint. Form the matrix of cofactors, then transpose it (flip rows↔columns). That's the adjoint, $\text{adj}(A)$.

4 · Divide by the determinant. $A^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{\det A}\text{adj}(A)$. This last division is the same $\tfrac15$ idea — and again shows why $\det A=0$ blocks the inverse.

Try it

2 $\det A=1$, so $A^{-1}=\frac{1}{1}\begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{bmatrix}$.

Part II

Rank, Null Space & Echelon Form

L06

Matrix Rank

How many genuinely independent directions a matrix carries.

Rank
rank = number of linearly independent rows (= columns)
= the dimension of the space the matrix spans
Why we need it

Rank measures the amount of genuine, non-redundant information in a matrix. In data science, if two features are really the same information in disguise, they don't add a new dimension — rank tells you how many truly independent features (or equations) you have.

Where it comes from

Each row (or column) points in some direction. If a row can be built from the others by scaling and adding (a linear combination), it adds nothing new — it's redundant. Rank simply counts the rows that do add a new independent direction. Geometrically that's the dimension of the space the matrix's rows/columns span — a line is dimension 1, a plane dimension 2, and so on.

Each term, decoded
indep.
Linearly independent
No row/column is a combination of the others.
span
Span
All points reachable by combining the rows/columns.
!
Square-matrix link: an $n\times n$ matrix has full rank $n$ exactly when $\det\neq0$ — that's the same as being invertible. Lose rank → determinant becomes 0 → no inverse.
Try it

$\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{bmatrix}$.

1 Row 2 = 2× row 1 → only 1 independent row → rank = 1 (not 2).

L07

Echelon Form

The "staircase" form that makes rank obvious.

Row Echelon Form
$$\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{1}&*&*\\0&\mathbf{1}&*\\0&0&\mathbf{0}\end{bmatrix}$$
Why we need it

Reading rank or independence straight off a messy matrix is hard. Echelon form is a tidy, standardized state you reach with simple row operations — and once a matrix is in it, the rank is just the count of non-zero rows. It also makes solving linear systems straightforward.

Where it comes from

Using row operations (swapping rows, scaling a row, adding a multiple of one row to another — none of which change the rank), you arrange the matrix into a staircase:

• Each row's first non-zero entry (its pivot) sits further to the right than the pivot in the row above.

• Any all-zero rows sink to the bottom.

Each pivot marks one genuinely independent direction, so the number of pivots (= non-zero rows) is the rank.

pivot

The first non-zero number in a row; anchors a step of the staircase.
$*$
Any value
Entries to the right of a pivot can be anything.
$0$
Forced zeros
Everything below each pivot is cleared to zero.
Try it

$\begin{bmatrix}1&2&3\\2&4&6\\1&0&1\end{bmatrix}$.

1 $R_2-2R_1=(0,0,0)$; $R_3-R_1=(0,-2,-2)$.

2 Reorder → two non-zero rows → rank = 2.

L08

Null Space & Nullity

The directions a matrix collapses to zero — and what they reveal about data.

Null Space
$$\text{Null}(A)=\{\mathbf{x}\ :\ A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}\}$$
Why we need it

The null space captures redundancy. In a dataset, if you include both "Monthly Salary" and "Annual Salary," the second is just 12× the first — no new information. That kind of redundancy shows up as a non-trivial null space, and its size (the nullity) measures how much repeated/derivable information your matrix contains.

Where it comes from

Ask: which input vectors $\mathbf{x}$ does the matrix crush to the zero vector? Those are the solutions of $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$. If the only solution is $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$, nothing is being collapsed (no redundancy). But if non-zero $\mathbf{x}$ also map to $\mathbf{0}$, the matrix is throwing away directions — and the set of all such $\mathbf{x}$ forms the null space, a subspace. Its dimension is the nullity.

Try it

A 2-feature matrix where column 2 = 12 × column 1 (Monthly vs Annual salary).

1 Rank = 1 (one real direction), 2 columns → nullity = 1.

2 That nullity of 1 = the one redundant feature.

L09

Rank vs Nullity — Information & Redundancy

Every column is either new information or repeated information.

Rank–Nullity Theorem
$$\operatorname{rank}(A)+\operatorname{nullity}(A)=\text{number of columns}$$
Why we need it

It splits a matrix's content into two clean buckets: rank = useful, independent information and nullity = redundant information. Together they account for every column. This instantly tells you how compressible a dataset is and whether a linear system has a unique or infinitely-many solutions.

Where it comes from

Think of each column as one unit of input. When the matrix acts, that column either adds a genuinely new output direction — counting toward the rank — or it's a combination of others and collapses to zero, contributing a direction to the null space and counting toward nullity. Every column lands in exactly one bucket, so the two must sum to the total number of columns. It's conservation of dimensions.

Redundant information
Directions collapsed to zero.
find it columns − rank

All input dimensions.
Try it

A matrix with 5 columns and rank 3.

1 nullity $=5-3=2$ → 3 columns carry real info, 2 are redundant.

The same object, seen through three different lenses.

One vector, three views

Why we need it

"Vector" means slightly different things in different fields, and data science borrows from all three. Seeing the connections lets you use geometric intuition (from physics) on what is really just a list of numbers (computer science), backed by rigorous rules (mathematics).

L11

Vector Operations

Adding vectors, and stretching/shrinking/flipping them.

Addition & scalar multiplication
$$\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}=(a_1+b_1,\ a_2+b_2)\qquad c\mathbf{a}=(ca_1,\ ca_2)$$
Why we need it

Addition and scalar multiplication are the only two operations a vector space is built on — every other idea (linear combinations, span, basis) is made from them. Geometrically they're how you move around and rescale within a space.

Where it comes from

Addition — combine two vectors component-by-component. Visually, place the tail of $\mathbf b$ at the head of $\mathbf a$ (the head-to-tail method); the arrow from start to finish is $\mathbf a+\mathbf b$. Equivalently, the parallelogram law: $\mathbf a+\mathbf b$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram they form.

Scalar multiplication — multiply every component by a number $c$. This stretches the vector when $|c|>1$, shrinks it when $|c|<1$, and reverses its direction when $c<0$ — but it never tilts it off its line.

Each term, decoded
$\mathbf a,\mathbf b$
The vectors
Added component-by-component.
$c$
A scalar
A plain number. $|c|>1$ stretch · $|c|<1$ shrink · $c<0$ flip.
Try it

$\mathbf a=(1,2)$, $\mathbf b=(3,1)$.

1 $\mathbf a+\mathbf b=(4,3)$.

2 $-2\mathbf a=(-2,-4)$ — twice as long and reversed.

L12

Linear Combinations

Mixing building-block vectors to reach new points.

Linear Combination
$$\mathbf{v}=x\mathbf{a}+y\mathbf{b}$$
Why we need it

A linear combination is how you reach new vectors from a few you already have. This single idea underlies span (everything you can reach), linear dependence (when one vector is redundant), and basis (a minimal reaching-set) — the backbone of the whole subject.

Where it comes from

It's just the two basic operations used together: scale each vector ($x\mathbf a$, $y\mathbf b$) and then add the results. By choosing the weights $x$ and $y$, you can land on many different points. A vector $\mathbf v$ "is a linear combination of $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$" exactly when some choice of $x,y$ produces it.

L13

Basis & Dimension

The minimal set of vectors that can reach everywhere.

Standard Basis of ℝ²
$$\hat{\imath}=(1,0)\qquad \hat{\jmath}=(0,1)$$
Why we need it

A basis is a set of independent building-block vectors from which every point in the space can be reached by a linear combination. It gives you a coordinate system, and the number of vectors in it — the dimension — tells you how many independent directions the space has.

Where it comes from

You want a reaching-set that is both sufficient (spans the whole space — you can get anywhere) and not wasteful (linearly independent — no vector is redundant). That "just enough, no more" set is a basis. The standard basis vectors $\hat{\imath},\hat{\jmath}$ are the simplest choice — unit arrows along each axis — so any vector $(x,y)=x\hat{\imath}+y\hat{\jmath}$ reads straight off its coordinates.

Part IV

Geometric Concepts & Machine Learning

L14

Hyperplanes

One equation that means "line," "plane," or higher — depending on dimension.

Hyperplane equation
$$\mathbf{w}^{T}\mathbf{x}+b=0$$
Why we need it

A hyperplane is a flat boundary that splits a space into two sides. It's the central object in linear classifiers (like SVMs): one side is "class A," the other "class B." The same formula works in any dimension, which is why it's written generally.

Where it comes from

The expression $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x$ is a dot product — it measures how far the point $\mathbf x$ extends along the direction $\mathbf w$. Setting $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x+b=0$ picks out exactly the points at one fixed "level" in that direction, which forms a flat sheet perpendicular to $\mathbf w$. In 2D that sheet is a line, in 3D a plane, in 4D+ a hyperplane — same equation, the dimension just changes how we picture it.

L15

Binary Classification & Half-Spaces

Which side of the boundary a point lands on.

The sign decides the class
$$\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}+w_0>0\ \to\ \text{positive side}\qquad <0\ \to\ \text{negative side}$$
Why we need it

A hyperplane only draws the boundary; classification needs a rule for which side a point is on. That rule is just the sign of the hyperplane expression — turning geometry into a yes/no decision, the core of linear classifiers.

Where it comes from

On the boundary, $\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf x+w_0=0$. Step to one side and the value becomes positive; step to the other side and it becomes negative. These two regions are the half-spaces. So you plug a point in: positive → predict the positive class, negative → the negative class. The further from zero, the further from the boundary (and the more confident the call).

L16

Distance to a Plane

How far a point sits from the boundary — including from the origin.

General & origin distance
$$d=\frac{|\mathbf{w}^{T}\mathbf{x}_0+b|}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}\qquad d_{\text{origin}}=\frac{|w_0|}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}$$
Why we need it

How confidently a point belongs to its class depends on how far it is from the boundary. The distance is what SVMs maximize (the "margin"), and the special case from the origin is a common quick computation.

Where it comes from

Plug the point into the plane expression: $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x_0+b$. This is zero on the plane and grows as you move away — but it's also inflated by how long $\mathbf w$ is. Divide by $\|\mathbf w\|$ to strip out that scaling and recover the true perpendicular distance. The absolute value keeps it positive regardless of side.

For the distance from the origin, set $\mathbf x_0=\mathbf 0$: the $\mathbf w^T\mathbf x_0$ term vanishes, leaving just $\dfrac{|w_0|}{\|\mathbf w\|}$ (here $w_0$ is the bias term).

L17

The Dot Product

Angle between vectors, and projection of one onto another.

Dot Product
$$\mathbf{a}^{T}\mathbf{b}=\sum_i a_ib_i=\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$$
Why we need it

The dot product is a single number that tells you how aligned two vectors are — used to find the angle between them, to project one vector onto another, and to test for perpendicularity. It's the most-used operation across machine learning.

Where it comes from

Two equal faces. The computational form $\sum a_ib_i$ ("multiply matching components, add") is easy to calculate. The geometric form $\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|\cos\theta$ shows the meaning: the lengths multiplied, scaled by the cosine of the angle between them. Same direction ($\theta=0$) → large positive; perpendicular ($\theta=90°$, $\cos=0$) → zero; opposite → negative. Rearranging also gives the projection of $\mathbf a$ onto $\mathbf b$.

L18

Cosine Similarity

Comparing vectors by direction alone — the engine behind AI embeddings.

Cosine Similarity
$$\cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|}$$
Why we need it

Often only direction matters, not size. In AI, text is turned into vectors called embeddings; two passages on the same topic should count as similar even if one is far longer (a bigger vector). Cosine similarity ignores length and compares pure orientation — which is exactly how systems decide if two text chunks are related.

Where it comes from

Take the geometric dot product $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=\|\mathbf a\|\|\mathbf b\|\cos\theta$ and solve for $\cos\theta$ by dividing out both lengths. Dividing by the magnitudes normalizes the vectors to unit size, so what remains is only the cosine of the angle — a clean score from −1 (opposite) through 0 (unrelated) to +1 (same direction), independent of how long the vectors are.

L19

The Transpose

Flipping a matrix across its diagonal.

Transpose & its key property
$$(A^{T})_{ij}=a_{ji}\qquad (AB)^{T}=B^{T}A^{T}$$
Why we need it

The transpose turns rows into columns, which is needed to write dot products as $\mathbf a^T\mathbf b$, to define symmetric matrices ($A=A^T$), and to build the covariance matrices and normal equations that show up throughout statistics and machine learning.

Where it comes from

You simply reflect the matrix across its main diagonal: the entry in row $i$, column $j$ moves to row $j$, column $i$. An $m\times n$ matrix becomes $n\times m$. The important rule is that transposing a product reverses the order: $(AB)^T=B^TA^T$ — like putting on socks then shoes, undoing reverses the sequence.

L20

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

The directions a matrix only stretches — found via the characteristic equation.

Eigen-equation → characteristic equation
$$A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\ \Longrightarrow\ \det(A-\lambda I)=0$$
Why we need it

When a matrix acts, most vectors get knocked off their direction. A special few — the eigenvectors — keep pointing the same way and merely get scaled by a factor $\lambda$. Those directions are the "natural axes" of a transformation and power PCA (directions of greatest variance), PageRank, and stability analysis.

Where it comes from

We want a non-zero $\mathbf v$ that the matrix only stretches: $A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v$. Rearrange to $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf v=\mathbf 0$ (the identity $I$ lets us subtract the scalar $\lambda$ from the matrix). This says $(A-\lambda I)$ crushes a non-zero vector to zero — which a matrix can only do if it's singular, i.e. its determinant is 0. So solve the characteristic equation $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ for the $\lambda$ values, then substitute each back to find its eigenvector.

Try it

$A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}$.

1 $\det(A-\lambda I)=(4-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-2=\lambda^2-7\lambda+10$.

2 $(\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)=0\Rightarrow\lambda=5,2$.

L21

Trace & Determinant via Eigenvalues

Two free shortcuts that double as error checks.

Eigenvalue relations
$$\sum\lambda_i=\operatorname{trace}(A)\qquad \prod\lambda_i=\det(A)$$
Why we need it

These two relations let you find or verify eigenvalues without fully solving, and they're the fastest way to catch arithmetic mistakes in an eigenvalue problem — if your eigenvalues don't add to the trace and multiply to the determinant, you slipped somewhere.

Where it comes from

The characteristic equation $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ is a polynomial whose roots are the eigenvalues. By the standard relationship between a polynomial's roots and its coefficients, the sum of the roots equals the sum of the diagonal entries (the trace), and the product of the roots equals the constant term, which works out to the determinant. So both facts fall straight out of the characteristic polynomial.
